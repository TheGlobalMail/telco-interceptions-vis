Drug Money

CHRIS HOPKINS

Dr Tim Read is bombarded by promotional material from pharmaceutical companies.

The Doctor’s Dilemma

By Mike Seccombe, Clare Blumer, Debra JopsonApril 29, 2013

By law medical professionals must keep up their education — providing plenty of opportunities for wealthy pharmaceutical companies to help out. But do free flights, consultancy fees, free dinners and free conferences put doctors in Big Pharma’s pocket?

Dr Tim Read doesn’t think the free pens which litter his surgery compromise his medical independence. It’s not as though a cheap biro, particularly one advertising the maker of a herpes drug, is a terribly desirable gift.

“I try not to look at the logo,” he jokes.

He has supped at the table of Big Pharma, too. Not often, not lavishly, just enough to constitute what he calls “an ever-so-slight culinary conflict of interest”.

But such potential conflicts are all but unavoidable for medical professionals in Australia. From the free pens, to the flights to medical conferences, to the lucrative invitations to participate in ‘consultation panels’ and ‘advisory boards’, Big Pharma seems ubiquitous in the lives of doctors, nurses, pharmacists and other medical professionals.

To continue practicing, they are required by law to pursue ongoing professional education, to keep them up to date with the latest best practice. And the pharmaceutical industry provides and supports plenty of educational opportunity.

“Postgraduate medical education,” says Read, “is riddled with, and appears to be dependent on, pharmaceutical money.

“Go to any GP or specialist conference. It will have a gold sponsor or a platinum sponsor and a whole bunch of bronze sponsors, who will have paid $5,000 or $20,000 or whatever, depending on what metal they’re named after.

“When you walk in, you go to one lecture hall or another, and between each speaker, there’s a slide that comes up naming the sponsors. And then when you go in for your very nice lunch, there’s a whole lot of little stalls — like a trade fair — not just little desks with paper but a good cappuccino machine and a queue of doctors at it — and, I admit, I’ve drunk some. And there are gimmicks and giveaways — all sorts of stuff. It’s like going to the motor show, a pleasant assault on the senses.

“That’s the face of post-graduate medical education. There are drugs in the air you’re breathing.”

And constant dilemmas for an ethical medical professional.

Says Melbourne-based Read: “No-one really cares about a sandwich or a pen, but it’s very hard to know where it should stop.”

For this particular doctor, it stops just the other side of the sandwich.

“I avoid accepting airfares,” he says. “It’s a long time since I’ve been to a drug company-sponsored dinner.”

He has been invited, as a specialist in sexual health and HIV/AIDS, to take part in extremely well-paid ‘consultancies’, and has declined.

As he jokingly put it: “I’m not clean, but I’m not that much of a slut.”

Many other health professionals draw the line somewhere else.

They accept the free airline tickets to Paris, New York, wherever, and the expensive wining and dining. They take the lucrative consultancies that Big Pharma offers, and the speaking gigs. They are happy to do it without informing their patients. And they do it, presumably, secure in the belief that there is nothing untoward in dipping into the pool of money Big Pharma has to splash around on conferences, meals, functions, airfares, sponsorships, donations, grants, consultancies, education and sundry other means by which the drug companies seek to influence them to use more of their products.

And it is a vast pool of money. According to the limited data supplied by companies to the pharmaceutical industry’s peak body, Medicines Australia, and collated by The Global Mail, more than $300 million has been spent on “educational events” alone since reporting began in 2007 — that’s $1 million a week plus some change.

On average, there are 83 such events every day, staged for the benefit not just of doctors, but also pharmacists, nurses and other medical professionals. These range, as the Medicines Australia website says, “from small meetings in surgeries to evening briefings over dinner, to a weekend conference for hundreds of healthcare professionals”.

The great majority of these hospitality-provided “educational” events are held in Australia, mostly in the three big eastern states, but the most valued medical professionals might be invited to France or Germany or the US or UK, or one of about 40 other overseas conference destinations.

The average cost of these educational events is not so great — about $70 a head — but they represent just the tip of the pharmaceutical industry influence-peddling iceberg.

It’s a huge business, the drugs biz. According to the most recent numbers, released on April 3 by Medicines Australia, the pharma industry turned over just under $22.5 billion in 2010-11. And it spends big, promoting the drugs it produces, in just about every way you can imagine — to doctors, to patients, to government.

Armies of sales representatives go direct to doctors and into hospitals to push their wares. Lobbyists walk the corridors of Canberra doing the same.

There are CDs, DVDs, USBs, podcasts, prescribing software and websites, and smartphone apps.

There are so-called “starter packs” and “product familiarisation programs” — essentially free supplies of products — direct grants and other financial support, loans and gifts of medical equipment, and services such as “clinical audits” of practices. There is money given to patient groups and medical charities. Not to mention direct advertising in medical publications.

“I’m not clean, but I’m not that much of a slut.”
− Dr Tim Read

In all, billions of dollars are spent on promotion. According to Ray Moynihan, senior research fellow at Bond University and trenchant critic of drug company practice: “That [$300 million spent on educational events] is only a small portion of the marketing spend. It’s important, but it’s only little.

“A very, very large portion is the so-called detailing. That is the rather attractive men and women who go and visit your GP and your specialist regularly.

“We haven’t seen those figures revealed.

“A very large amount goes on free samples which go into GP and specialist surgeries which are an incredibly effective, and some would say dangerous marketing tool, because they provide an instant distortion to prescribing practice, because the new product is sitting there, a way of getting both doctor and patient excited about the new product, which ultimately will cost a lot more, once the free samples run out.

“Obviously all the advertising purchased in medical journals and the like is another big expenditure [as is] all of the so-called disease awareness campaigns and the PR companies associated with them, which directly influence media coverage. Again, we have no figures on all of that.

“And the flying of people around the world, when there’s no hospitality involved, there’s a bucket of money there too.

“Those are just a few of the things not covered by the so-called educational event budget you’re looking at.”

Now, not all of this is necessarily bad. Some of it does genuinely inform medical professionals about treatment alternatives. What it is, unquestionably, is opaque, and riddled with the possibility of malpractice of one kind or another.

The View from Big Pharma

Just a year ago GlaxoSmithKline copped the biggest fine for medical fraud ever in the United States, USD 3 billion, for, in the late 1990s to mid-2000s, promoting several drugs for uses for which they had not been approved and for withholding data on the serious health risks of one drug.

While the penalty in the GSK case was unprecedentedly large, the case was by no means unique, or even uncommon, in type.

One of the world’s most-respected medical journals, The Lancet, quoted Macchiavelli when it editorialised on the “moral decay” at GSK:

“…‘he who seeks to deceive will always find someone who will allow himself to be deceived’. Sadly, in the case of GSK, this has involved doctors who were all too ready to be beguiled by illegal kickbacks and lavish hospitality.”

Like many other commentaries on the ethics of Big Pharma, The Lancet editorial questioned whether companies were even much chastened by the penalties levied against them, or whether they looked upon them simply as a business expense.

“Multibillion-dollar fines are inadequate to tackle misconduct when penalties can be paid from post-tax profits,” it said.

“GSK and other drug companies have come adrift from the standards of the societies they seek to serve. They need to rediscover corporate citizenship…”

Alas, similar scandals continue to pop up at frequent intervals, in various jurisdictions around the world.

As for GSK, the company said publicly at the time the misconduct occurred in a different era and would not be tolerated. its 2012 annual report to shareholders, under the heading ‘Risk factors’, subhead ‘potential litigation’ (pp 84-85), mentioned the $3 billion fine but expressed no contrition. Rather, it whinged about the difficulty of getting insurance to cover such liabilities. It flagged the possibility of having to make “additional significant provisions related to legal proceedings and investigations which would reduce its earnings”.

While The Lancet cites lofty ideals, there is evidence from Wall Street to Fleet Street that competition in the absence of effective regulation brings lower standards of propriety.

And so, in America, the Obama administration has moved to stiffen regulation of pharmaceutical company practices. From the start of August this year, under the Sunshine Act, drug companies must publish the names of all doctors and teaching hospitals that receive benefits from pharma companies. Doctors who receive benefits exceeding $10, or $100 in aggregate in a year, must be named and published on a government website. Penalties of up to $1.5 million apply for transgressions. (Medicines Australia’s maximum fine for breaching its code is $300,000, recently raised from $200,000.)

Reportable benefits in the US include speaking fees, gifts, honoraria, food and drink, entertainment, education, travel and accommodation, royalties, investments, grants, research payments and more. A complete guide is available here.

In addition to the far more robust system of penalties already applying to pharma companies, these new requirements on medical professionals put the US light years ahead of Australia in terms of regulation.

The good news is, some steps are being taken towards greater transparency in Australia.

Exposing the payments made to doctors was on the agenda when Medicines Australia in late 2012 updated its code of conduct. But the proposal met with opposition. Much of the stonewalling came from the doctors’ groups.

MA and the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC) settled for the compromise of publishing yearly totals of payments. But the ACCC, pointing to America’s Sunshine Act, put the issue back on the table almost immediately; the regulator granted only a two-year approval for the code of conduct, instead of the five years sought by Medicines Australia.

MA responded by creating a Transparency Working Group, to answer the ACCC’s demands for the next code of conduct in 2015.

According to the most recent numbers, the pharma industry turned over just under $22.5 billion in 2010-11.

“That group has been charged with looking at how to have further transparency in that relationship and make some recommendations,” says chief executive, Dr Brendan Shaw.

Shaw says the task force has brought diverse members of the sector into the group, for their input and “so they could go back to their respective sectoral groups and have a think what that might mean for them.”

One aspect of all this which seems, at first blush, surprising, is that the pharma companies, or some of them at least, are well ahead of the doctors when it comes to transparency.

Then again, perhaps it’s not so surprising. As GSK’s Lisa Maguire (associate director, corporate affairs and patient advocacy) concedes, more transparency can only improve the reputation of the pharma companies.

She says her company would like to implement greater disclosure policies globally. The company has made the case in Canberra for more transparent and detailed reporting.

In the wake of that record USD3 billion fine in America, GSK is intent on improving its reputation for ethical and open practice. To that end, says Maguire: “We absolutely support individual disclosure,” she says.

And that disclosure needs to be collated and easily accessible, she adds, because patients “don’t want to have to go to the MA website and trawl through stuff. You don’t want to have to look at 10 different pharmaceutical companies and look up your doctor’s name.”

As things now stand in Australia though, not only does Medicines Australia only provide aggregate figures on benefits from companies to doctors, but membership of MA is voluntary and many companies are not even part of it. Many other companies provide the information in very opaque form, making it difficult to collate.

Says GSK’s Maguire: “The only way to achieve that greater confidence in the industry is through every single company doing it.”

That’s one stumbling block. Another, she says, is that “it’s not just about individual companies. It involves a lot of other people.”

For “other people”, read “doctors”.

The View From the Doctor’s Office

Individually, many doctors would like to see transparency.

Tim Read is one. Richard Di Natale, health spokesman for the Greens, is another.

In February this year, Dr Di Natale introduced a bill in federal parliament which would have severely curtailed the benefits pharma companies could bestow on doctors, and mandated disclosure of “payments made to doctors, including fees, gifts, honoraria, services such as travel and accommodation, donations or research grants”. The doctors would be named.

His attempt at forcing greater disclosure, albeit not as strict as the US, is yet to be debated; the first hearing is today, April 29, in Melbourne. The chances of the bill getting anywhere do not seem good. Apart from anything else, no-one in the major parties ever wants to give the Greens a win.

Ella Rubeli/The Global Mail

Some of Dr Paul Wilson’s pens that are branded with herpes medication logos — some of the less desirable Big Pharma gifts.

Collectively, though, doctors have resisted such transparency. The doctors’ union, the Australian Medical Association, has long seen big downsides to greater transparency.

Unlike the big drug companies, whose poor reputations can only improve through transparency, doctors could suffer damage to their reputations if the public comes to see them as being on the take.

Still, the AMA is moving away from its former strong opposition to disclosure, says its president, Dr Steve Hambleton. “Since I’ve started as vice president and now president, our view has changed,” he says. “Clearly we’re moving towards individual disclosure.”

On the subject of what exactly should be disclosed, though, he talks in rather vague terms about applying “the common sense test”.

“I don’t think anyone needs to know if someone bought you a sandwich at lunchtime, or a coffee,” he says.

On the other hand, if a doctor is earning 50 per cent of his or her income through consultancies for a particular drug company, he suggests, “that’s the sort of thing Medicines Australia might say is something people need to know about, and whether it is influencing their decisions.”

The reforms, when they come, will start “at the big end of town” says Hambleton. He does not specify but the obvious inference is that means speaking fees and consultancies, rather than free pens and sandwiches.

Even there, though, he has qualms. Highly-qualified specialists are in demand to speak at educational events, and their time is expensive.

“We don’t want to undermine confidence in the professor of orthopedics because he went to a conference overseas to do some education,” says Hambleton.

“If a professor running the best cardiology group in Australia has to go overseas to Boston to present findings, and it’s paid for by a pharmaceutical company … we don’t want people thinking, ‘Oh, he’s off to Boston; he’s on the take’.”

Says Hambleton: “The prime focus for the AMA is to maintain [patients’] confidence the doctor is working on their behalf, and there’s no third party in the room, so to speak. They need to know a decision is being made because of their clinical condition, not because their doctor has a relationship with a pharmaceutical group.”

A recent survey of 2,756 physicians in Australasia showed nearly all had provided paid advice to the pharmaceutical industry.

The research also included focus groups with physicians. Public-health-policy specialist Mary Osborn, who conducted the research, says that a great number of the doctors admitted feeling obliged to return the favour in some way.

“If your doctor recommends a drug, most people are loath to say, ‘I’m worried about why you’re giving this to me, because I see you take money from this company.’ I mean it’s basically calling your doctor a crook.”
− Prof Nicholas Rosenlicht

“If you’ve got a registrar funded by a pharmaceutical industry that promotes kidney medicines — I’m being very careful of how I say this,” says Osborn, “because of the gifts, it’s reciprocity, they’re bound to be influenced, and they will use those drugs more often. There’s so much evidence around that now.”

Of the pharmaceutical companies’ lavish spending on doctors’ education, Dr Tim Read says, “They must reckon that it buys influence. It’s shareholders’ money; it’s being spent to increase the share price.”

What It Looks Like To The Public

No question doctors do pretty well out of the arrangement. Exactly how well is difficult to say, given the paucity of publicly available data. But Read points to some of the common benefits, over and above the free sandwiches and meals at educational events:

One widespread practice is for companies to pay conference registration for doctors, maybe $500 or $1,000. And often, particularly if you’re coming from out of state and/or are in a field of practice where you write expensive prescriptions, they’ll fly you in, and put you up, he says.

“Fortunately specialists like myself in public employment don’t need rely on that so much, because many of our awards give us generous funding to use for registrations and trips to conferences and things like that.”

In Victoria, where he works, that allowance is worth $20,000 a year for a doctor working full-time.

Consumer advocate Carol Bennett says, “The focus has now shifted more to health professionals and their role in all of this.

“There has always been the sense... that the industry is at fault in a lot of this, and very often it has been,” said Bennett, the CEO of Consumer Health Forum. “But the [health care] providers are the other half of the equation, that until now have largely escaped attention and any sort of responsibility.

“Now the spotlight’s being thrown onto their role ... closing off the loopholes that allow them to exploit the opportunities the industry gives them.”

The onus to declare conflicts of interest must fall on doctors and corporations, says University of California professor of psychiatry Nicholas Rosenlicht.

“If your doctor recommends a drug, most people are loath to say, ‘I’m worried about why you’re giving this to me, because I see you take money from this company.’ I mean it’s basically calling your doctor a crook,” he tells The Global Mail.

So patients do not ask about conflicts of interest.

“Virtually never. No, never... People don’t do that. You only go to a doctor because you’re hurt, because something is wrong. Why would you want to attack somebody that you’re asking help from?”

General practitioner Dr Evan Ackermann decided eight years ago to stop attending drug-company-sponsored events and seeing pharmaceutical representatives when they drop in to his Queensland rural practice.

“Do I regret it? No. I’ll make up for it in other ways,” said Ackermann, who is the chair of the national standing committee on quality care for the Royal Australian College of General Practitioners.

“You do miss out on some of the new product coming out that may be different... A classic thing is a new pill [that] came out that had a new delivery mechanism to help patients. That’s something that’s worthwhile knowing,” he said, and such information doesn’t always get before a doctor otherwise.

He would find the gifts of branded physical-aids useful (such as models of knees to show patients), but that is not enough to change his mind and accept gifts again.

He’s not alone among doctors who believe pharmaceutical companies’ insidious intrusion into their professional lives is unhealthy.

The Doctors’ Reform Society argues we could do well without them. Its policy holds that because of the conflicts “between rational use of treatments and the commercial imperatives of manufacturers”, all medical education should be entirely independent of drug manufacturers.

“The DRS supports government funding of such independent education.”

Auckland-based psychiatry academic, David Menkes, told The Global Mail that pharmaceutical companies have “a vital role to play in drug development and bringing products on the market, but they’ve got no business educating us about how to practice, how to use those products.

“If we need education we need to source that and organise it ourselves.

“That’s not to say that we can’t talk with them, but to have those people with a very clear vested interest and an overt commercial agenda come in and educate doctors,” Menkes said, “it’s unnecessary, embarrassing and compromises the integrity of our profession — it’s just nuts.”

After 4 years of not seeing Reps, reading how we are subliminally and overtly influenced, I'm not turning back. I have no doubt that unbalanced viewpoints from funded Specialists, drug sponsored meetings and branded office items do influence prescribing in an environment of multiple drug choice. Either, we reinforce the independent resources of drug information or restrict prescribing to established evidence based cost effective treatments. The latter would be unweildy and restrictive so let's have more non Pharma information funding.

June 6, 2013 @ 6:59am
by Robert

I have not seen drug reps or accepted drug company funding for about three years now. But I am disturbed by the recent federal budget that will cap self education costs to $2000 per year. Given that Doctors must attend about 50 hours of self education a year to remain registered the pendulum could swing more towards accepting drug sponsored events because of the large costs of independent teaching events. Just to register for a conference can be over $1000, let alone the cost of travel, accommodation and lost working time. Formal workshops can be over $10,000. Are we encouraging the dumbing down of our medical workforce?

by cameron

Great thought-provoking article. As an Australian, now practicing in the US, I look back at our practices in Australia and realize we were far more open to accepting Pharma representation compared to current allowances in the United States. But surely some of the concern here is born from the ideal that the medical profession was (and I hope still is) a place of moral high-ground and gravitas where trust in one's doctor is not only paramount but well placed. Other professions have never come under the same scrutiny as doctors - we never ask our financial advisor whether they taken out to dinner by their client, our plumber whether the tile manufacturer gave them a kickback or quiz our city engineer whether have they been to lunch meetings with prospective developers. We almost assume it has occurred. I shudder to think the influence, both recognised and silent, that occurs behind the doors of parliament. As a physician, I choose to analytically sift through the research literature I read, and the literature that arrives at my door, with exactly the same degree of critical skepticism. Without a careful consideration of data and evidence we are all prone to undue influence. I probably don't do it perfectly, but one has to try. So whilst I'm proud of my profession and the honourable light within which it stands, it becomes challenging when Medicine is examined under a different light; as if in some way doctors are felt less capable of balancing the same industry pressures applied to them. It almost belittles the profession.
